The Three Big Quantities

Voltage, Current, and Resistance

Concept

Given:

  • $V$ = Voltage (volts, V) — the "push" the battery provides.
  • $I$ = Current (amperes, A) — how fast charge flows through the wire.
  • $R$ = Resistance (ohms, Ω) — how much the bulb/wire resists the flow.

These three are tied together by Ohm's Law:

$$V = I \cdot R \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad I = \frac{V}{R}$$

💡 Think of it like water in a hose: voltage is the water pressure, current is how fast the water flows, and resistance is a kink in the hose.

Series vs. Parallel — The Core Difference

Series Circuits

Concept

Components are connected end-to-end, one after another, so the current has only one path to flow.

⚡ Same current through every component.

🔋 Voltage splits across components.

➕ Resistances add: $R_{\text{total}} = R_1 + R_2 + \dots$

Consequence for bulbs: adding more bulbs in series makes every bulb dimmer, because total resistance goes up → current drops.

⚠ Watch out: if one bulb in series burns out, the whole circuit breaks — just like an open switch!

Parallel Circuits

Concept

Components are connected on separate branches, so the current has multiple paths.

🔋 Same voltage across every branch.

⚡ Current splits between branches.

➕ Resistances add reciprocally: $\displaystyle \frac{1}{R_{\text{total}}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \dots$

Consequence for bulbs: adding more bulbs in parallel keeps each bulb at full brightness, but the battery drains faster because total current goes up.

💡 Real world: the lights in your house are wired in parallel, so turning one off doesn't turn the others off.

3

Batteries: Series vs. Parallel

Batteries in Series

$$V_{\text{total}} = V_1 + V_2 + \dots$$

Voltages add. Two 1.5 V batteries in series → 3 V total. Bulbs get brighter (maybe too bright).

Batteries in Parallel

$$V_{\text{total}} = V_1 = V_2$$

Voltage stays the same, but the batteries last longer because the load is shared.

1

Formula Reference

Ohm's Law

$$V = I \cdot R$$

V = voltage (V), I = current (A), R = resistance (Ω). The master equation — rearrange to find any one of the three.

Electrical Power

$$P = I \cdot V = I^2 R = \frac{V^2}{R}$$

P = power in watts (W). How fast electrical energy turns into light and heat. Bigger P → brighter bulb.

Series Resistance

$$R_{\text{series}} = R_1 + R_2 + R_3 + \dots$$

Resistances just add up. More bulbs in series → more total resistance → less current.

Parallel Resistance

$$\frac{1}{R_{\text{parallel}}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \dots$$

Reciprocals add. More bulbs in parallel → less total resistance (weird but true!).

Two Parallel Resistors (shortcut)

$$R_{\text{parallel}} = \frac{R_1 \cdot R_2}{R_1 + R_2}$$

Quick shortcut when you only have two branches. "Product over sum."

Energy Used

$$E = P \cdot t$$

E in joules (J) when t is in seconds. Power × time = total energy used.

Practice Problems

Try these with pencil & paper first, then check your answer. You can also verify them with the Circuit Lab tab!

1

Given: A 6 V battery is connected to a single 3 Ω bulb.
Find: the current $I$ through the bulb and the power $P$ it dissipates.

Given: $V = 6\text{ V}$, $R = 3\text{ Ω}$.

Use Ohm's law: $I = V/R = 6/3 = 2\text{ A}$.

Power: $P = IV = (2)(6) = 12\text{ W}$.

$$I = 2\text{ A},\quad P = 12\text{ W}$$
2

Given: Two 2 Ω bulbs in series with a 6 V battery.
Find: total resistance, current, and voltage across each bulb.

Given: $V = 6\text{ V}$, $R_1 = R_2 = 2\text{ Ω}$, series.

Series resistances add: $R_{\text{total}} = 2 + 2 = 4\text{ Ω}$.

Current: $I = V/R = 6/4 = 1.5\text{ A}$.

Same current goes through each bulb, so voltage across each: $V_1 = IR_1 = (1.5)(2) = 3\text{ V}$ (the 6 V split evenly).

$$R_{\text{total}} = 4\text{ Ω},\quad I = 1.5\text{ A},\quad V_{\text{each}} = 3\text{ V}$$
3

Given: Two 2 Ω bulbs in parallel with a 6 V battery.
Find: total resistance, total current from the battery, and current through each bulb.

Given: $V = 6\text{ V}$, $R_1 = R_2 = 2\text{ Ω}$, parallel.

Parallel resistance: $\displaystyle R_{\text{total}} = \frac{R_1 R_2}{R_1 + R_2} = \frac{(2)(2)}{2+2} = 1\text{ Ω}$.

Total current: $I_{\text{total}} = V/R = 6/1 = 6\text{ A}$.

Each branch gets the full 6 V, so current through each bulb: $I_{\text{branch}} = 6/2 = 3\text{ A}$. (Check: $3+3 = 6$ ✓)

$$R_{\text{total}} = 1\text{ Ω},\quad I_{\text{total}} = 6\text{ A},\quad I_{\text{branch}} = 3\text{ A}$$
4

Given: Comparing problems 2 and 3 (same battery, same two bulbs, just wired differently).
Find: which wiring (series or parallel) makes each bulb brighter, and explain why using power.

Series: each bulb has $I = 1.5\text{ A}$, so $P = I^2R = (1.5)^2(2) = 4.5\text{ W}$ per bulb.

Parallel: each bulb has $I = 3\text{ A}$, so $P = I^2R = (3)^2(2) = 18\text{ W}$ per bulb.

Parallel wins by a lot. Each parallel bulb gets the full 6 V, while each series bulb only gets 3 V. Since $P = V^2/R$, doubling the voltage quadruples the power — that's why the parallel bulbs are 4× brighter!

$$P_{\text{series}} = 4.5\text{ W},\quad P_{\text{parallel}} = 18\text{ W}\text{ per bulb}$$
5

Given: You have four 1.5 V batteries in series and one 3 Ω bulb.
Find: total voltage, current through the bulb, and the power dissipated. Would the bulb (rated for 1 W) survive?

Given: 4 batteries × 1.5 V in series, $R = 3\text{ Ω}$.

Series batteries add: $V = 4 \times 1.5 = 6\text{ V}$.

Current: $I = V/R = 6/3 = 2\text{ A}$.

Power: $P = IV = (2)(6) = 12\text{ W}$.

The bulb is rated for 1 W but we're pushing 12 W through it — it would burn out! 💥

$$V = 6\text{ V},\quad I = 2\text{ A},\quad P = 12\text{ W} \gg 1\text{ W} \;\Rightarrow\; \text{burnout}$$
