Key Terms

Electric Field $\vec{E}$
Force per unit charge at a point in space. It tells you the force a +1 C test charge would feel. Like gravitational acceleration $\vec{g}$, but for charge instead of mass. Units: N/C.
Point Charge $q$
A charge concentrated at a single point. Can be positive (source of field lines) or negative (sink of field lines). Measured in coulombs (C). $1\;\mu\text{C} = 10^{-6}$ C.
Coulomb's Constant $k_e$
$k_e = 8.99 \times 10^9$ N·m²/C². The electric equivalent of $G$ in gravity. Determines how strongly charges interact at a given distance.
Electric Potential $V$
Potential energy per unit charge — a scalar (no direction). Units: volts (V). Equipotential lines connect points of equal $V$ and are always perpendicular to field lines.
Electric Field Lines
Visual representation of the field. They start on + charges and end on − charges. Denser lines = stronger field. Lines never cross. The tangent at any point gives the field direction.
Superposition Principle
The net field at any point is the vector sum of the individual fields from each charge: $\vec{E}_{net} = \vec{E}_1 + \vec{E}_2 + \cdots$. Add components, not magnitudes!
2

Key Equations

Coulomb's Law

$$F = k_e\frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2}$$

Force between two point charges. Always use magnitudes for $F$; determine direction from signs.

Electric Field (point charge)

$$E = k_e\frac{|Q|}{r^2}$$

Field created by source charge $Q$ at distance $r$. Direction: away from + , toward −.

Electric Potential

$$V = k_e\frac{q}{r}$$

Scalar — keep the sign of $q$. Positive charge → positive $V$, negative charge → negative $V$.

Force from Field

$$\vec{F} = q\,\vec{E}$$

The electric version of $\vec{F} = m\vec{g}$. Positive $q$ feels force along $\vec{E}$; negative $q$ feels force opposite $\vec{E}$.

3

Electric Field Line Rules

Origin & Termination
Field lines start on positive charges and end on negative charges. If there's no nearby opposite charge, lines extend to infinity.
Direction (Tangent Rule)
The tangent to a field line at any point gives the direction of $\vec{E}$ at that point. The arrow on the line shows which way a positive test charge would move.
Density = Strength
Where field lines are packed closer together, the field is stronger. Spread-out lines mean a weaker field. Think of it like a crowd — packed = intense.
No Crossing
Field lines never intersect. At each point in space, $\vec{E}$ has a single unique direction. If lines crossed, there'd be two directions at one point — impossible.
Perpendicular to Equipotentials
Field lines always cross equipotential surfaces at right angles. Equipotential lines connect points of equal $V$; the field points from high $V$ to low $V$.
Symmetry
A spherically symmetric charge produces radial field lines, equally spaced in all directions. The number of lines is proportional to the magnitude of the charge.
4

Superposition Principle

$$\vec{E}_{net} = \vec{E}_1 + \vec{E}_2 + \vec{E}_3 + \cdots = \sum_{i} k_e \frac{q_i}{r_i^2}\,\hat{r}_i$$

How to apply it:

  1. Identify all source charges and the point $P$ where you want $\vec{E}_{net}$.
  2. For each charge, calculate $E_i = k_e |q_i| / r_i^2$ and determine the direction (away from + , toward −).
  3. Break each $\vec{E}_i$ into $x$ and $y$ components.
  4. Add all $x$-components: $E_x = \sum E_{ix}$. Add all $y$-components: $E_y = \sum E_{iy}$.
  5. Recombine: $|\vec{E}_{net}| = \sqrt{E_x^2 + E_y^2}$, direction: $\theta = \arctan(E_y / E_x)$.
Common mistake: Don't add magnitudes directly! $|\vec{E}_1| + |\vec{E}_2| \neq |\vec{E}_{net}|$ unless the fields point in the same direction. You must add as vectors.
1

The Big Idea: Fields as "Force-per-Stuff"

A Field is Just "Acceleration Waiting for a Mass"

You already know the equation $F = ma$. Rearrange it:

$$a = \frac{F}{m}$$

Acceleration is force per unit mass. It tells you: "if you put a 1 kg object here, this is how fast it accelerates."

The electric field works exactly the same way, but for charge instead of mass:

$$\vec{E} = \frac{\vec{F}}{q}$$

Electric field is force per unit charge. It tells you: "if you put a 1 C charge here, this is the force it feels." Units: N/C

Just like acceleration exists at every point in a gravitational field whether or not a mass is there, the electric field exists at every point in space whether or not a charge is there to feel it. The field is the "potential for force" — add a charge, and the force appears.

2

Electricity & Gravity: The Same Story Twice

The electric force and the gravitational force have almost identical structures. Every equation in electricity has a gravitational twin — just swap charge for mass and $k_e$ for $G$.

Step 1 — The Force Laws

Electric Force (Coulomb's Law)

$$F_e = k_e\frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}$$

$k_e = 8.99 \times 10^9$ N·m²/C²

Gravitational Force (Newton's Law)

$$F_g = G\frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}$$

$G = 6.674 \times 10^{-11}$ N·m²/kg²

Step 2 — Divide Out the Test Object to Get the Field

Electric Field

$$\vec{E} = \frac{\vec{F}_e}{q} = k_e\frac{Q}{r^2}$$

Units: N/C (force per unit charge)

Gravitational Field

$$\vec{g} = \frac{\vec{F}_g}{m} = G\frac{M}{r^2}$$

Units: N/kg = m/s² (it's acceleration!)

Step 3 — Force from Field

Electric Force on a Charge

$$\vec{F}_e = q\,\vec{E}$$

Charge feels force along (or against) the field

Gravitational Force on a Mass

$$\vec{F}_g = m\,\vec{g}$$

Mass feels force along the field (always toward source)

Step 4 — Potential (Energy per "Stuff")

Electric Potential

$$V = k_e\frac{q}{r}$$

Units: V (joules per coulomb). Scalar — keeps sign of $q$.

Gravitational Potential

$$\Phi = -G\frac{M}{r}$$

Units: J/kg. Always negative (gravity is always attractive).

Complete Translation Table

Every concept in one column has a direct counterpart in the other. When you learn something new about electric fields, you already know the gravitational version — and vice versa.

Coupling constant $k_e = 8.99 \times 10^9$ $G = 6.674 \times 10^{-11}$

Field $\vec{E} = k_e \frac{Q}{r^2}$ $\vec{g} = G \frac{M}{r^2}$
Force from field $\vec{F} = q\vec{E}$ $\vec{F} = m\vec{g}$
Potential $V = k_e \frac{q}{r}$ $\Phi = -G\frac{M}{r}$

Superposition? Yes — vector sum of all $\vec{E}_i$ Yes — vector sum of all $\vec{g}_i$
4

The Pattern in One Picture

Here's the logic flow that connects everything. This same chain of reasoning works for both gravity and electricity:

Source creates a field
Charge $Q$ creates $\vec{E} = k_e Q/r^2$
Mass $M$ creates $\vec{g} = GM/r^2$
Field exerts force on "test stuff"
$\vec{F} = q\vec{E}$
$\vec{F} = m\vec{g}$
Potential = stored energy per "stuff"
$V = k_e q/r$
$\Phi = -GM/r$
Multiple sources? Add up the fields (superposition)
$\vec{E}_{net} = \sum \vec{E}_i$
$\vec{g}_{net} = \sum \vec{g}_i$
Bottom line: $F = mg$ and $F = qE$ are the same equation wearing different hats. The number 9.81 is just Earth's gravitational field $\vec{g}$ evaluated at the surface. When you learn Coulomb's Law, you're really learning Newton's Law of Gravitation again — with charge playing the role of mass.
5

Where the Analogy Breaks Down

The gravity-electricity parallel is powerful, but there are real differences. Understanding where the analogy stops working is just as important as seeing where it works.

Difference #1 — Sign of the source
Mass is always positive, so gravity is always attractive. Charge can be positive or negative, giving both attraction and repulsion. This is why there are no gravitational "dipoles."
Difference #2 — Strength
$k_e \approx 10^9$ vs. $G \approx 10^{-11}$. Electricity is roughly $10^{20}$ times stronger than gravity at the same distance for elementary particles. We don't notice because everyday objects are electrically neutral — the positive and negative charges cancel out.
Difference #3 — Potential sign convention
Electric potential $V = k_e q/r$ keeps the sign of $q$ and can be positive or negative. Gravitational potential $\Phi = -GM/r$ is always negative (the minus sign is a convention reflecting that you need to add energy to escape).
Despite these differences, the math is the same. If you can solve an electric field problem, you already know how to solve the gravitational version — just swap $q \leftrightarrow m$ and $k_e \leftrightarrow G$ (and remember gravity only attracts).
6

So What is 9.81 Really?

You've been using $g = 9.81$ m/s² since your first physics class. But now you can see where it actually comes from — it's just the gravitational field evaluated at Earth's surface:

Givens:

$G = 6.674 \times 10^{-11}$ N·m²/kg² (gravitational constant)

$M_{\text{Earth}} = 5.972 \times 10^{24}$ kg

$R_{\text{Earth}} = 6.371 \times 10^{6}$ m

$$g = G\frac{M_{\text{Earth}}}{R_{\text{Earth}}^2} = (6.674 \times 10^{-11})\frac{5.972 \times 10^{24}}{(6.371 \times 10^6)^2} \approx 9.81 \text{ m/s}^2$$

That's it. The number $9.81$ is not a fundamental constant — it's the result of plugging Earth's mass and radius into the field equation. On the Moon ($M$ smaller, $R$ smaller), you'd get $g \approx 1.62$ m/s². On Jupiter, $g \approx 24.8$ m/s². Try it with the gravity visualizer on the first tab!

Single Point Charge Field

A point charge of $+8.0$ µC is located at the origin. What is the magnitude of the electric field at a distance of 0.5 m from the charge?

Electric Field

Givens: $q = +8.0 \;\mu\text{C} = 8.0 \times 10^{-6}$ C, $r = 0.5$ m, $k_e = 8.99 \times 10^9$ N·m²/C²

$$E = k_e\frac{|q|}{r^2} = (8.99 \times 10^9) \frac{8.0 \times 10^{-6}}{(0.5)^2} = (8.99 \times 10^9)(3.2 \times 10^{-5})$$ $$\boxed{E = 2.88 \times 10^5 \text{ N/C, directed radially outward}}$$
2

Net Field from Two Charges on an Axis

Two charges are on the $x$-axis: $q_1 = +3.0$ µC at $x = 0$ and $q_2 = -2.0$ µC at $x = 4.0$ m. Find the magnitude and direction of the net electric field at $x = 2.0$ m.

Superposition (1D)

Givens: $q_1 = +3.0\;\mu$C at $x=0$, $q_2 = -2.0\;\mu$C at $x=4.0$ m, point $P$ at $x=2.0$ m

From $q_1$: $r_1 = 2.0$ m, field points rightward (away from +)

$$E_1 = k_e\frac{3.0 \times 10^{-6}}{(2.0)^2} = 6.74 \times 10^3 \text{ N/C} \;\rightarrow$$

From $q_2$: $r_2 = 2.0$ m, field points rightward (toward −)

$$E_2 = k_e\frac{2.0 \times 10^{-6}}{(2.0)^2} = 4.50 \times 10^3 \text{ N/C} \;\rightarrow$$

Both fields point right! (away from + and toward −)

$$\boxed{E_{net} = 6.74 \times 10^3 + 4.50 \times 10^3 = 1.12 \times 10^4 \text{ N/C (rightward)}}$$
3

Force on a Charge in a Known Field

An electric field of $\vec{E} = 5000$ N/C points due east. A charge of $q = -2.0\;\mu$C is placed in this field. What is the magnitude and direction of the force on the charge?

Force from Field

Givens: $E = 5000$ N/C (east), $q = -2.0 \times 10^{-6}$ C

$$F = |q| \cdot E = (2.0 \times 10^{-6})(5000) = 0.01 \text{ N}$$

Since $q$ is negative, the force is opposite to $\vec{E}$.

$$\boxed{F = 0.01 \text{ N, directed due west}}$$
4

Superposition in 2D (Right Triangle)

Charge $q_1 = +4.0\;\mu$C sits at the origin and $q_2 = +3.0\;\mu$C sits at $(3,\,0)$ m. Find the net electric field at point $P = (3,\,4)$ m.

Vector Superposition

Givens: $q_1 = +4.0\;\mu$C at origin, $q_2 = +3.0\;\mu$C at $(3,0)$, point $P = (3,4)$

From $q_1$: $r_1 = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = 5$ m, $\theta_1 = \arctan(4/3) = 53.13°$

$$E_1 = k_e\frac{4.0 \times 10^{-6}}{25} = 1.44 \times 10^3 \text{ N/C}$$ $$E_{1x} = 1.44 \times 10^3 \cos 53.13° = 864 \text{ N/C}$$ $$E_{1y} = 1.44 \times 10^3 \sin 53.13° = 1151 \text{ N/C}$$

From $q_2$: $r_2 = 4$ m, points straight up ($+y$)

$$E_2 = k_e\frac{3.0 \times 10^{-6}}{16} = 1.69 \times 10^3 \text{ N/C}$$ $$E_{2x} = 0, \quad E_{2y} = 1.69 \times 10^3 \text{ N/C}$$

Sum components:

$$E_x = 864, \quad E_y = 1151 + 1690 = 2841$$ $$\boxed{|\vec{E}_{net}| = \sqrt{864^2 + 2841^2} = 2970 \text{ N/C}, \quad \theta = \arctan\!\left(\frac{2841}{864}\right) = 73.1° \text{ from } +x}$$
5

Electric Potential at a Point

A point charge of $-4.0$ µC is at the origin. What is the electric potential at a distance of 0.2 m from the charge?

Electric Potential

Givens: $q = -4.0 \times 10^{-6}$ C, $r = 0.2$ m

$$V = k_e\frac{q}{r} = (8.99 \times 10^9) \frac{-4.0 \times 10^{-6}}{0.2} = (8.99 \times 10^9)(-2.0 \times 10^{-5})$$ $$\boxed{V = -1.80 \times 10^5 \text{ V} = -180 \text{ kV}}$$

The negative sign means a positive test charge would gain kinetic energy moving toward this point (it's being attracted to the negative charge).

6

Net Potential from Three Charges

Three point charges are arranged in a line: $q_1 = +2$ µC at $x = 0$, $q_2 = -3$ µC at $x = 2$ m, and $q_3 = +1$ µC at $x = 4$ m. Find the net electric potential at $x = 1$ m.

Scalar Superposition

Givens: $q_1 = +2\;\mu$C at $x=0$, $q_2 = -3\;\mu$C at $x=2$, $q_3 = +1\;\mu$C at $x=4$, point $P$ at $x=1$

$$V_1 = k_e\frac{2 \times 10^{-6}}{1} = 1.798 \times 10^4 \text{ V}$$ $$V_2 = k_e\frac{-3 \times 10^{-6}}{1} = -2.697 \times 10^4 \text{ V}$$ $$V_3 = k_e\frac{1 \times 10^{-6}}{3} = 2.997 \times 10^3 \text{ V}$$ $$\boxed{V_{net} = 1.798 \times 10^4 - 2.697 \times 10^4 + 2.997 \times 10^3 \approx -5.99 \times 10^3 \text{ V}}$$

Gravity Analogy: Surface Field of Mars

Mars has mass $M = 6.42 \times 10^{23}$ kg and radius $R = 3.39 \times 10^6$ m. Calculate the gravitational field strength at the surface of Mars. How does this compare to Earth's 9.81 m/s²?

Gravitational Field

Givens: $G = 6.674 \times 10^{-11}$ N·m²/kg², $M = 6.42 \times 10^{23}$ kg, $R = 3.39 \times 10^6$ m

$$g_{\text{Mars}} = G\frac{M}{R^2} = (6.674 \times 10^{-11})\frac{6.42 \times 10^{23}}{(3.39 \times 10^6)^2}$$ $$g_{\text{Mars}} = \frac{4.285 \times 10^{13}}{1.149 \times 10^{13}}$$ $$\boxed{g_{\text{Mars}} \approx 3.73 \text{ m/s}^2 \approx 0.38\, g_{\text{Earth}}}$$

Mars has about 38% of Earth's surface gravity. Same formula as $E = k_e Q/r^2$ — just swap charge for mass!

8

Conceptual: Field Lines Between Like Charges

Two equal positive charges are placed side by side. Describe the field line pattern. What happens at the exact midpoint between them?

Field Lines

Field lines originate from both positive charges and curve away from each other. Between the charges the lines repel, leaving a sparse region.

At the exact midpoint, the fields from each charge are equal and opposite, so:

$$\boxed{\vec{E}_{net} = \vec{0} \text{ at the midpoint}}$$

This is called a null point. No field lines pass through it. Try this in the visualizer!

9

Equipotentials and Work

A charge moves along an equipotential line from point $A$ to point $B$, both at $V = 200$ V. How much work is done by the electric field on the charge during this move?

Electric Potential

Key idea: Work done by the field equals charge times the change in potential:

$$W = q(V_A - V_B) = q(200 - 200) = 0$$ $$\boxed{W = 0}$$

Moving along an equipotential requires zero work — the field is perpendicular to the path. This is why equipotential lines are always perpendicular to field lines.
