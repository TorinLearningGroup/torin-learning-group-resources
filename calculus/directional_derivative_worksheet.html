Magnitude = maximum rate of change. Unit Direction Vector $$\mathbf{u} = \frac{\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|}$$ From angle: $\mathbf{u} = \langle \cos\theta, \sin\theta \rangle$ Directional Derivative: Dot Product $$D_{\mathbf{u}}f = \nabla f \cdot \mathbf{u}$$ Rate of change of $f$ per unit distance in direction $\mathbf{u}$. Directional Derivative: Geometric $$D_{\mathbf{u}}f = |\nabla f|\cos\theta$$ $\theta$ = angle between $\nabla f$ and $\mathbf{u}$. Alignment determines sign and size. 2 Geometric Meaning of the Gradient Part A: Gradient on a Surface Imagine $z = f(x,y)$ as a hill. The gradient $\nabla f = \langle f_x, f_y \rangle$ is an arrow that lives on the ground (the $xy$-plane) and answers: which way is steepest uphill, and how steep? $$\nabla f = \langle f_x, f_y \rangle \text{ points in direction of steepest increase}$$ $|\nabla f|$ equals the maximum slope magnitude at that point. Part B: Gradient and Level Curves The gradient is always perpendicular to the level curves of $f$. Level curves are the curves where $f(x,y) = c$ for different constants $c$. If you stand on a level curve and walk along it, $f$ doesn't change (by definition). The directional derivative along the level curve is zero: $$D_{\mathbf{u}}f = \nabla f \cdot \mathbf{u} = 0 \quad \text{when} \quad \nabla f \perp \mathbf{u}$$ This means the gradient must be perpendicular to the level curve (since zero directional derivative requires perpendicularity). $f=2$ $f=4$ $f=6$ $f=8$ $\nabla f$ tangent to curve $\nabla f \perp$ level curve Why this matters: Level curves are the contour lines on a map. The gradient at any point on the map always points perpendicular to the contour line at that point: straight toward higher elevation. Walking along the contour (perpendicular to the gradient) keeps you at the same height. Part C: Gradient and Tangent Planes (3D) For a surface written as $F(x,y,z) = 0$, the gradient $\nabla F = \langle F_x, F_y, F_z \rangle$ is normal (perpendicular) to the surface at any point. This gives the equation of the tangent plane: $$F_x(x_0,y_0,z_0)(x-x_0) + F_y(x_0,y_0,z_0)(y-y_0) + F_z(x_0,y_0,z_0)(z-z_0) = 0$$ The gradient vector at $(x_0,\,y_0,\,z_0)$ is the normal vector to the tangent plane. This is the 3D version of how the gradient is perpendicular to level curves in 2D. $xy$ $\nabla F = (f_x,\,f_y,\,-1)$ normal to tangent plane tangent plane curved surface $(f_x,\,f_y) = \nabla f$ shadow on $xy$-plane $xy$-plane THE NORMAL $\NABLA F$ PROJECTS ONTO THE $XY$-PLANE AS $\NABLA F$ Geometric bridge: The plane tangent to the surface at $P$ has a normal vector $\nabla F = (f_x,\,f_y,\,-1)$. Projecting that normal down onto the $xy$-plane drops the $-1$ component and leaves exactly $(f_x,\,f_y)$: the 2D gradient $\nabla f$. The gradient you see on the map is the shadow the surface normal casts down onto the ground. 3 From Maximum to Zero: Why Constraints Work Start from the directional derivative: $$D_{\mathbf{u}}f = \nabla f \cdot \mathbf{u} = |\nabla f|\,|\mathbf{u}|\cos\theta = |\nabla f|\cos\theta$$ Since $|\mathbf{u}| = 1$, the directional derivative is controlled entirely by $|\nabla f|$ and $\cos\theta$. Maximum: $\theta = 0$ $$D_{\mathbf{u}}f = |\nabla f| \cdot 1 = |\nabla f|$$ Move with the gradient: fastest increase. The maximum rate of change is $|\nabla f|$, and it occurs in the gradient direction. Minimum: $\theta = \pi$ $$D_{\mathbf{u}}f = |\nabla f| \cdot (-1) = -|\nabla f|$$ Move against the gradient: fastest decrease. ↓ Zero Change: $\theta = \pi/2$ $$D_{\mathbf{u}}f = |\nabla f| \cdot 0 = 0$$ Move perpendicular to the gradient: no change at all. This is the key to constrained optimization. 4 Constrained Movement Insight When movement is restricted by a constraint, feasible motion occurs only along the tangent direction to the constraint. At a constrained optimum: Directional derivative in every feasible direction = 0 This means the gradient must be perpendicular to the constraint curve. If it weren't, you could walk along the constraint and still go uphill. constraint peak optimum $\nabla f$ feasible feasible $\nabla f$ not optimal: improve by moving right $\nabla f$ not optimal: improve by moving left At the optimum: $\nabla f \perp$ constraint $D_{\mathbf{u}}f = 0$ in every feasible direction Reading the diagram: The concentric ellipses are level curves (contour lines) of the objective function, with the peak at the center. The dark curve is the constraint (the fence). At the gold dot (optimum), the gradient $\nabla f$ points straight toward the peak and is perpendicular to the constraint: moving along the fence gives $D_{\mathbf{u}}f = 0$. At the red dot, the gradient has a component along the fence, so you could still improve by walking along it. 5 Application: Consumer Optimization The Setup (No Econ Background Needed) Imagine you have a fixed budget of $I$ dollars to spend on two goods, $x$ and $y$. Each unit of $x$ costs $p_x$ and each unit of $y$ costs $p_y$. You want to be as happy as possible. We model your happiness with a function $U(x,y)$: the more of each good you have, the happier you are. A common choice is: $$U(x,y) = x^\alpha y^{1-\alpha} \qquad (0 < \alpha < 1)$$ Key: $U(x,y)$ = utility (happiness) from buying $x$ units of good 1 and $y$ units of good 2 $p_x, p_y$ = price per unit of each good $I$ = total income (your budget) $\alpha$ = how much you value good $x$ relative to good $y$ The budget constraint $p_x x + p_y y = I$ is a straight line in the $xy$-plane showing all the combinations you can afford. You want to find the point on this line where $U$ is highest. This is exactly the constrained movement problem from Section 4. The budget line is the constraint (the fence), and utility is the function we want to maximize (the mountain). Step 1: The feasible directions are tangent to the budget line. Step 2: At the optimum, $D_{\mathbf{u}}U = 0$ in every feasible direction. Step 3: This means $\nabla U$ is perpendicular to the budget line. Step 4: The budget line has direction $\langle -p_y, p_x \rangle$, so $\nabla U \cdot \langle -p_y, p_x \rangle = 0$. Step 5: Working this out gives the familiar condition: Good x Good y $U_3$ $U^*$ $U_1$ Budget: $p_x x + p_y y = I$ $I/p_y$ $I/p_x$ $\nabla U$ $x^*$ $y^*$ $\nabla U$ not optimal: improve by buying more Good x and less Good y optimum Indifference Curves Reading the diagram: Each curved line is an indifference curve: a level curve of the happiness function $U(x,y)$. Every point on the same curve gives equal happiness (different combinations of $x$ and $y$ that make you equally satisfied). Curves further from the origin represent higher happiness. The budget line shows what you can afford. The optimum is where the highest reachable indifference curve just touches the budget line: $\nabla U \perp$ budget, so $D_{\mathbf{u}}U = 0$ along the budget. Same idea as the constrained movement problem above: This is exactly the fence-and-mountain setup from Section 4. The budget line plays the role of the fence (you are constrained to walk along it). The utility function $U(x,y)$ plays the role of the mountain (you want to reach the highest point you can). At the red point, the directional derivative along the budget is nonzero: you can increase happiness by sliding along the constraint (buying more Good x and less Good y). At the gold optimum, $D_{\mathbf{u}}U = 0$ in every feasible direction, so $\nabla U$ is perpendicular to the budget line: there is no way to rearrange your spending and do better. $$\frac{MU_x}{p_x} = \frac{MU_y}{p_y}$$ Connection: The "equal bang for your buck" rule from introductory economics is just the directional derivative condition $D_{\mathbf{u}}U = 0$ along the budget constraint. Every dollar spent on $x$ gives the same marginal utility as every dollar spent on $y$. 6 Lagrange Multipliers: The Method In Sections 3–5 we saw why constrained optima require $\nabla f \perp$ constraint. Lagrange multipliers turn that geometric insight into an algebraic method you can solve. The Setup Given: $f(x,y)$ = objective function (what we want to maximize or minimize) $g(x,y) = k$ = constraint (the "fence" we must stay on) Key Condition: At a constrained optimum, $\nabla f$ is parallel to $\nabla g$, meaning: $$\nabla f = \lambda\,\nabla g$$ where $\lambda$ is the Lagrange multiplier. Why parallel? We showed that $\nabla f \perp$ constraint at the optimum. But $\nabla g$ is also perpendicular to the level curve $g = k$ (that's what gradients do). Two vectors both perpendicular to the same curve must be parallel — so $\nabla f = \lambda\,\nabla g$ for some scalar $\lambda$. The Algorithm Step 1: Write the system $$\nabla f = \lambda\,\nabla g \quad \text{and} \quad g(x,y)=k$$ This gives 3 equations in 3 unknowns ($x, y, \lambda$). Step 2: Solve $$f_x = \lambda\,g_x \quad f_y = \lambda\,g_y \quad g(x,y)=k$$ Solve this system for $x$, $y$, and $\lambda$. Often: isolate $\lambda$ from each gradient equation, set them equal, then substitute into the constraint. Step 3: Evaluate & compare $$\text{Plug candidates into } f(x,y)$$ The largest value is the constrained maximum; the smallest is the constrained minimum. Extension to Three Variables For $f(x,y,z)$ subject to $g(x,y,z) = k$: $$f_x = \lambda\,g_x, \quad f_y = \lambda\,g_y, \quad f_z = \lambda\,g_z, \quad g(x,y,z) = k$$ 4 equations, 4 unknowns ($x, y, z, \lambda$). Two Constraints For $f(x,y,z)$ subject to $g(x,y,z)=k_1$ and $h(x,y,z)=k_2$: $$\nabla f = \lambda\,\nabla g + \mu\,\nabla h$$ 5 equations ($3$ gradient + $2$ constraints), 5 unknowns ($x, y, z, \lambda, \mu$). What does $\lambda$ mean? The multiplier $\lambda$ measures the sensitivity of the optimal value to the constraint. If $f^*$ is the optimal value when the constraint is $g = k$, then $\lambda \approx \frac{df^*}{dk}$. In the consumer problem: $\lambda$ tells you how much extra utility one more dollar of income would buy — the marginal value of relaxing the constraint. 7 Reference: Gradient Rules Linearity $$\nabla(au+bv)=a\nabla u+b\nabla v$$ Product Rule $$\nabla(uv)=u\nabla v+v\nabla u$$ Quotient Rule $$\nabla\!\left(\frac{u}{v}\right) = \frac{v\nabla u - u\nabla v}{v^2}$$ Power Rule $$\nabla(u^n)=nu^{n-1}\nabla u$$ Torin Learning Group: Worksheet on Gradient, Directional Derivatives & Constrained Optimization For questions or feedback, visit us online.
