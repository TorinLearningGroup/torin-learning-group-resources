From Projection
to Gradient Descent

A step-by-step chain: how scalar components, directional derivatives, and surface normals connect to the algorithm behind machine learning.

Component→ Projection→ Directional Derivative→ Gradient→ Tangent Plane→ Surface Normal→ Gradient Flow→ Gradient Descent
1
Component vs. Projection
Vector foundation

Draw two arrows \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\). The question is: how much of \(\mathbf{b}\) points in the direction of \(\mathbf{a}\)?

Scalar component → a number
The signed length of \(\mathbf{b}\) in direction \(\mathbf{a}\). How long is the shadow?
Vector projection → an arrow
The actual arrow that is the part of \(\mathbf{b}\) lying along \(\mathbf{a}\). The shadow itself.
Definitions — let \(\mathbf{a},\,\mathbf{b}\) be vectors
\(\displaystyle \operatorname{comp}_{\mathbf{a}}\mathbf{b} = \frac{\mathbf{b}\cdot\mathbf{a}}{\|\mathbf{a}\|}\) scalar — the shadow length
\(\displaystyle \operatorname{proj}_{\mathbf{a}}\mathbf{b} = \frac{\mathbf{b}\cdot\mathbf{a}}{\|\mathbf{a}\|^2}\,\mathbf{a}\) vector — the shadow arrow
\(\mathbf{a}\)
\(\mathbf{b}\)
\(\operatorname{proj}_{\mathbf{a}}\mathbf{b}\)
\(\operatorname{comp}_{\mathbf{a}}\mathbf{b}\) (length)
scalar shadow
The shadow of \(\mathbf{b}\) onto \(\mathbf{a}\) — component is its length, projection is the arrow
Key distinction: Component is a number. Projection is a vector. One tells you how much, the other gives you the actual arrow.
2
The Gradient
Full slope description on a surface

Now imagine height is given by a function \(z = f(x,y)\) — a hill. The gradient is an arrow that lives on the ground and answers: which way is steepest uphill, and how steep?

Given \(f(x,y)\) — a surface
\(\displaystyle \nabla f = (f_x,\; f_y)\) points in the direction of steepest increase
\(\displaystyle \|\nabla f\|\) equals the maximum slope magnitude
\(\nabla f\)
\(z = f(x,y)\)
\(\nabla f\) on ground
steepest uphill
The gradient arrow lives in the \(xy\)-plane, pointing toward steepest ascent
3
Directional Derivative
Slope felt when walking in a chosen direction

Pick any unit direction vector \(\mathbf{u}\) on the ground. The slope you feel while walking that way is the directional derivative — and it is exactly the scalar component of \(\nabla f\) in direction \(\mathbf{u}\).

Given \(\nabla f = (f_x, f_y)\), unit direction \(\mathbf{u}\), \(\|\mathbf{u}\| = 1\)
\(\displaystyle D_{\mathbf{u}}f = \nabla f \cdot \mathbf{u}\) scalar — slope felt in direction \(\mathbf{u}\)
\(\displaystyle (\nabla f \cdot \mathbf{u})\,\mathbf{u}\) vector projection — slope arrow along \(\mathbf{u}\)
This is the Step 1 connection: the directional derivative is the scalar component formula from Step 1, with \(\nabla f\) playing the role of \(\mathbf{b}\) and \(\mathbf{u}\) playing the role of \(\mathbf{a}\).

The directional derivative is the component of \(\nabla f\) in the walking direction — same formula as Step 1
4
Tangent Plane
The local linear model of the surface

Zoom in close enough on any smooth surface and it looks flat — a tangent plane. This plane contains all possible local slope directions; the gradient picks out the steepest one within it.

Near point \((x_0,\,y_0)\), with \(z_0 = f(x_0,y_0)\)
\(\displaystyle z \approx z_0 + f_x(x - x_0) + f_y(y - y_0)\)
All local slope directions live inside this tangent plane. The gradient direction within the plane gives the steepest climb.
5
Surface Normal Connection
The gradient is the horizontal shadow of the 3D normal

Rewrite the surface as a level surface of a 3D function. The 3D gradient is perpendicular to the surface — a normal vector. The gradient you've been drawing on the ground is its horizontal shadow.

Rewrite surface as level set: \(F(x,y,z) = f(x,y) - z = 0\)
\(\displaystyle \nabla F = (f_x,\; f_y,\; -1)\) perpendicular to the surface (3D normal)
Horizontal projection of \(\nabla F\):  \(\displaystyle (f_x,\;f_y) = \nabla f\) the planar gradient
\(\nabla F = (f_x,\,f_y,\,-1)\)
normal to tangent plane
tangent plane

\((f_x,\,f_y) = \nabla f\)

The plane tangent to the surface at \(P\) has a normal vector \(\nabla F = (f_x, f_y, -1)\). Projecting that normal down onto the \(xy\)-plane drops the \(-1\) component and leaves exactly \((f_x, f_y)\) — the 2D gradient \(\nabla f\).
Geometric bridge: the gradient you see on the map is the shadow the surface normal casts down onto the ground.
6
Steepest Descent Direction
Flip the gradient, get downhill

The gradient maximizes the directional derivative — it points steepest uphill. So the direction of fastest decrease is simply its negation.

Maximizing the directional derivative over all unit directions
\(\displaystyle \max_{\|\mathbf{u}\|=1}\;\nabla f \cdot \mathbf{u} = \|\nabla f\|\) achieved when \(\mathbf{u} = \nabla f\,/\,\|\nabla f\|\)
\(\displaystyle -\nabla f\) direction of steepest decrease
Imagine placing a marble on the hill and letting it go. It rolls in the direction \(-\nabla f\).
7
Gradient Flow
Continuous downhill motion — a differential equation

To minimize \(f\), imagine a particle that continuously slides downhill. This gives a differential equation called gradient flow. Its solutions trace paths that always head toward local minima.

Gradient flow ODE
\(\displaystyle \frac{d\mathbf{x}}{dt} = -\nabla f(\mathbf{x})\) continuous motion in the steepest downhill direction
minimum
\(\mathbf{x}(0)\)
\(\tfrac{d\mathbf{x}}{dt} = -\nabla f(\mathbf{x})\)
continuous slide down into minimum
Gradient flow: the particle slides down the bowl toward the minimum
8
Gradient Descent
Euler's method applied to gradient flow

We can't solve the gradient flow ODE continuously — so we approximate it numerically using Euler's method: take a small discrete step downhill, re-evaluate the slope, then step again.

Euler's method for ODE \(\dot{\mathbf{x}} = g(\mathbf{x})\)
\(\displaystyle \mathbf{x}_{k+1} = \mathbf{x}_k + h\,g(\mathbf{x}_k)\) \(h\) = step size
Substitute \(g(\mathbf{x}) = -\nabla f(\mathbf{x})\) — gradient descent
\(\displaystyle \mathbf{x}_{k+1} = \mathbf{x}_k - \alpha\,\nabla f(\mathbf{x}_k)\) \(\alpha\) = learning rate
minimum
\(\mathbf{x}_0\)
- - - gradient flow (continuous)
●— gradient descent (discrete steps)
Gradient descent (gold) approximates gradient flow (dashed) with discrete steps of size \(\alpha\)
The punchline: Gradient descent is Euler's method applied to gradient flow. Every step, evaluate the local slope \(\nabla f(\mathbf{x}_k)\) and step against it by \(\alpha\). That is the algorithm behind neural network training.
The Full Chain
01Component: \(\operatorname{comp}_{\mathbf{a}}\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{b}\cdot\mathbf{a}/\|\mathbf{a}\|\): scalar shadow length
02Projection: \(\operatorname{proj}_{\mathbf{a}}\mathbf{b} = (\mathbf{b}\cdot\mathbf{a}/\|\mathbf{a}\|^2)\,\mathbf{a}\): shadow arrow
03Directional derivative: \(D_{\mathbf{u}}f = \nabla f \cdot \mathbf{u}\): component of \(\nabla f\) in walking direction
04Gradient: \(\nabla f = (f_x, f_y)\): full steepest-slope vector on the ground
05Tangent plane: \(z \approx z_0 + f_x(x-x_0) + f_y(y-y_0)\): local linear model
06Surface normal: \(\nabla F = (f_x, f_y, -1)\) perpendicular to surface; \(\nabla f\) is its horizontal shadow
07Steepest descent: \(-\nabla f\) points downhill
08Gradient flow: \(d\mathbf{x}/dt = -\nabla f(\mathbf{x})\): continuous downhill ODE
09Gradient descent: \(\mathbf{x}_{k+1} = \mathbf{x}_k - \alpha\,\nabla f(\mathbf{x}_k)\): Euler's method on gradient flow
Worked Examples

Two concrete problems tracing the full chain from gradient to gradient descent step.

Example 1 — Paraboloid
Find the gradient, directional derivative, and one gradient descent step
Given: \(f(x,y) = x^2 + y^2\), starting point \(\mathbf{x}_0 = (3,\,4)\), walking direction \(\mathbf{u} = \bigl(\tfrac{3}{5},\,\tfrac{4}{5}\bigr)\), learning rate \(\alpha = 0.1\).
Step 1 — Compute the gradient
\(\displaystyle \nabla f = (f_x,\,f_y) = (2x,\,2y)\)
\(\displaystyle \nabla f\big|_{(3,4)} = (6,\,8)\) this arrow points steepest uphill at \((3,4)\)
Step 2 — Directional derivative in direction u
\(\displaystyle D_{\mathbf{u}}f = \nabla f \cdot \mathbf{u} = (6,8)\cdot\Bigl(\tfrac{3}{5},\tfrac{4}{5}\Bigr) = \tfrac{18}{5} + \tfrac{32}{5} = 10\) slope of 10 when walking in direction \(\mathbf{u}\)
Step 3 — One gradient descent step
\(\displaystyle \mathbf{x}_1 = \mathbf{x}_0 - \alpha\,\nabla f(\mathbf{x}_0) = (3,4) - 0.1\cdot(6,8) = (2.4,\;3.2)\)
\(\displaystyle f(\mathbf{x}_0) = 25 \qquad f(\mathbf{x}_1) = 5.76 + 10.24 = 16\) function value dropped from 25 to 16
The minimum of \(f(x,y)=x^2+y^2\) is at \((0,0)\). Each step of gradient descent moves closer — the gradient points outward (uphill), so we step inward.
Example 2 — Saddle surface
Gradient, steepest direction, tangent plane, and normal vector
Given: \(f(x,y) = x^2 - y^2\), point \(P = (2,\,1)\).
Step 1 — Gradient at P
\(\displaystyle \nabla f = (2x,\,-2y) \quad\Rightarrow\quad \nabla f\big|_{(2,1)} = (4,\,-2)\) points in steepest uphill direction on the saddle
\(\displaystyle \|\nabla f\| = \sqrt{4^2 + (-2)^2} = \sqrt{20} = 2\sqrt{5}\) maximum slope magnitude
Step 2 — Tangent plane at P, where \(z_0 = f(2,1) = 3\)
\(\displaystyle z \approx 3 + 4(x-2) + (-2)(y-1) = 4x - 2y - 3\)
Step 3 — Surface normal vector at P
\(\displaystyle \nabla F = (f_x,\,f_y,\,-1)\big|_{(2,1)} = (4,\,-2,\,-1)\) perpendicular to the tangent plane
Horizontal shadow: \(\displaystyle (4,\,-2) = \nabla f\) the 2D gradient is the projection of the 3D normal onto \(xy\)
Step 4 — One gradient descent step with \(\alpha = 0.05\)
\(\displaystyle \mathbf{x}_1 = (2,1) - 0.05\cdot(4,-2) = (1.8,\;1.1)\)
\(\displaystyle f(2,1) = 3 \qquad f(1.8,\,1.1) = 3.24 - 1.21 = 2.03\) function decreased toward the saddle point
On a saddle \(f = x^2 - y^2\), gradient descent finds the saddle point \((0,0)\) where \(\nabla f = \mathbf{0}\), but this is not a true minimum — it is a critical point. This is why second-order methods and careful initialisation matter in practice.
Same point (0,0), two cross-sections: a bowl in x (looks like a min) but an arch in y (actually a max) — so (0,0) is neither a minimum nor a maximum
