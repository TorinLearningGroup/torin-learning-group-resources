What is Constrained Optimization?

Many real-world problems ask us to maximize or minimize a function $f(x,y)$ or $f(x,y,z)$ not freely, but subject to a restriction on the variables. For example: maximize volume of a box given a fixed surface area, or find the point on a surface closest to a given point.

The General Problem: Find the extreme values of $f(x,y)$ subject to the constraint $g(x,y) = 0$, where the constraint defines a curve (or surface in 3D) on which we must stay.
2

The Complete Two-Check System

To find all candidates for extrema on a constraint, you must check two separate sets of points:

Check 1 — Lagrange Candidates

Solve $\nabla f = \lambda \nabla g$ together with $g = 0$. This finds all smooth, regular points where extrema can occur.

Valid only where $\nabla g \neq \vec{0}$ and $g$ is differentiable.

Check 2 — Singular & Boundary Points

Find all points where $\nabla g = \vec{0}$ or where $g$ is not differentiable (endpoints, corners, cusps).

Lagrange is blind here — check manually by evaluating $f$.
Why two checks? Lagrange multipliers rely on $\nabla g$ pointing in a well-defined normal direction. Wherever that geometry breaks down — cusps, endpoints, singular points — Lagrange gives no information. The true extremum can hide at exactly these places.

These two checks cover every point on the constraint:

$$\underbrace{\text{all points on constraint}}_{\text{everything}} = \underbrace{\text{smooth regular points}}_{\text{Check 1: Lagrange}} \cup \underbrace{\{\nabla g = \vec{0}\}}_{\text{algebraic singularities}} \cup \underbrace{\{g \text{ not differentiable}\}}_{\text{boundary / endpoints}}$$
Key insight: Checks 1 and 2 are genuinely non-overlapping. Check 1 requires $g$ to be differentiable with $\nabla g \neq \vec{0}$. Check 2 catches everything else. Together they are complete.
3

Why $\nabla g = \vec{0}$ Means a Singular Point

Normally, $\nabla g$ at a point on the constraint tells you which direction is "outward" — it is perpendicular (normal) to the constraint curve. The Lagrange argument says: at an extremum, $\nabla f$ must be parallel to this normal, hence $\nabla f = \lambda \nabla g$.

When $\nabla g = \vec{0}$, the constraint has lost its geometry at that point. There is no well-defined tangent line or normal direction. The entire logical foundation of Lagrange collapses before it even starts.

Cusp (e.g. piriform at origin)
The curve approaches from two directions that are not parallel — two conflicting tangents. No unique normal exists, so $\nabla g = \vec{0}$.
Cone vertex (e.g. $z^2 = x^2 + y^2$ at origin)
The surface pinches to a sharp point. No tangent plane, no normal direction, $\nabla g = \vec{0}$.
Domain endpoint (e.g. $\sqrt{x} + \sqrt{y} = 5$ at $(25,0)$)
$g$ is not differentiable at all — $\sqrt{x}$ has no derivative at the boundary of its domain. Lagrange never gets a chance to apply.
1

The Geometric Idea

Imagine the level curves of $f$ — the curves where $f = c$ for various constants $c$. As $c$ increases, these curves sweep across the plane. We want the largest (or smallest) $c$ such that the level curve $f = c$ still touches the constraint $g = 0$.

Key geometric fact: The level curve $f = c$ touches the constraint $g = 0$ at an extremum if and only if they are tangent there — they share the same tangent line. If the level curve crosses the constraint, $f$ is still increasing or decreasing along the constraint, so we have not reached an extremum yet.

Two smooth curves are tangent if and only if their normal directions are parallel. The normal to $f = c$ is $\nabla f$, and the normal to $g = 0$ is $\nabla g$. Parallel normals means:

$$\nabla f = \lambda \nabla g \quad \text{for some scalar } \lambda$$
2

The Formal Theorem

Lagrange Multiplier Theorem: If $f$ has a local extremum at $\mathbf{p}$ on the constraint $g = 0$, and the regularity condition $\nabla g(\mathbf{p}) \neq \vec{0}$ holds, then there exists a scalar $\lambda$ such that: $$\nabla f(\mathbf{p}) = \lambda \nabla g(\mathbf{p})$$

This is a necessary condition, not sufficient. Every smooth extremum satisfies it, but not every solution is an extremum — saddle points satisfy it too.

Logical DirectionStatementTrue?
$\Rightarrow$Smooth extremum $\Rightarrow$ satisfies $\nabla f = \lambda \nabla g$Yes — theorem
$\Leftarrow$Satisfies $\nabla f = \lambda \nabla g$ $\Rightarrow$ is an extremumNo — could be saddle
3

Crossing vs. Tangency — Saddle Points

When the Lagrange system produces a candidate that is neither the max nor the min, it is a constrained saddle point. This happens when the level curve of $f$ crosses the constraint rather than kissing it.

True extremum (tangency): All nearby constrained points lie on the same side of the level curve — all have $f \geq c$ (min) or all have $f \leq c$ (max).
Saddle point (crossing): Nearby constrained points exist on both sides — some with $f > c$, some with $f < c$. Cannot be a max or min.

Lagrange detects tangency only to first order (gradient level). Whether a curve crosses or merely touches is a second order (curvature) phenomenon, which is why Lagrange cannot distinguish extrema from saddle points on its own.

4

Completeness Guarantee

Lagrange is complete on the smooth part: At any smooth, regular point on the constraint ($\nabla g \neq \vec{0}$, $g$ differentiable), if an extremum exists there, Lagrange will find it. It cannot miss any smooth extremum.
5

Extension to 3D and Two Constraints

Two constraints in 3D: To optimize $f(x,y,z)$ subject to $g(x,y,z) = 0$ and $h(x,y,z) = 0$, solve: $$\nabla f = \lambda \nabla g + \mu \nabla h$$ along with both constraint equations. This gives 5 equations in 5 unknowns $(x, y, z, \lambda, \mu)$.
1

Step-by-Step Algorithm

  1. Identify $f$ and $g$
    Write the constraint as $g(x,y) = 0$ (move everything to one side).
  2. Compute gradients
    Find $\nabla f = \langle f_x, f_y \rangle$ and $\nabla g = \langle g_x, g_y \rangle$ symbolically.
  3. Set up the Lagrange system
    Write $\nabla f = \lambda\,\nabla g$ component-by-component, plus $g = 0$.
    This gives $n+1$ equations in $n+1$ unknowns $(x, y, \ldots, \lambda)$.
  4. Solve the system
    Eliminate $\lambda$ (see strategies below), find a relationship between the variables, then substitute back into the constraint.
  5. Find singular / boundary points separately
    Solve $\nabla g = \vec{0}$, and check any points where $g$ is not differentiable (endpoints, domain edges).
    Lagrange is blind at these points — they must be checked by hand.
  6. Evaluate $f$ at every candidate
    Compare all values — Lagrange candidates and manually-found points alike.
  7. Classify
    Largest $f$ = maximum. Smallest $f$ = minimum. Anything in between = constrained saddle point.
2

What $\lambda$ Tells You

$\lambda$ is a rate of change

$\lambda \approx df/dc$, where $c$ is the constraint level. It measures how much the optimal value of $f$ would change if the constraint were relaxed by a small amount.

Reading the sign of $\lambda$
$\lambda > 0$
$\nabla f$ and $\nabla g$ point the same direction
$\lambda < 0$
$\nabla f$ and $\nabla g$ point in opposite directions
$\lambda = 0$
Unconstrained optimum already lies on the constraint
3

Common Solving Strategies

A — Eliminate $\lambda$

Solve each component equation for $\lambda$, then set them equal. This gives a direct relationship between $x$ and $y$. Substitute into the constraint.

B — Divide Equations

Divide one component equation by another to cancel $\lambda$. Only valid when denominators are nonzero — always check!

C — Factor First

When you see $2x(1-\lambda)=0$, factor immediately into cases: $x=0$ or $\lambda=1$. Handle each case with the constraint separately.

D — Parametrize

For circular constraints, substitute $x=r\cos t$, $y=r\sin t$ to reduce everything to single-variable calculus.

Closest Point on a Cone

Problem 1

Find the points on the cone $z^2 = x^2 + y^2$ closest to the point $(4, 2, 1)$.

Givens

  • Objective: minimize $f = (x-4)^2 + (y-2)^2 + (z-1)^2$
  • Constraint: $g = x^2 + y^2 - z^2 = 0$

Lagrange System

$$2(x-4) = 2\lambda x, \quad 2(y-2) = 2\lambda y, \quad 2(z-1) = -2\lambda z$$

Solving

From equations 1 and 2: $\lambda = 1 - 4/x = 1 - 2/y$, giving $y = x/2$. Substituting into the constraint: $z = \pm\frac{\sqrt{5}}{2}x$. Solving gives two candidate points.

Rectangle of Maximum Area

Problem 4 — Proof

Use Lagrange multipliers to prove that the rectangle with maximum area for a given perimeter $p$ is a square.

Givens

  • $x, y$ = side lengths of the rectangle
  • Objective: maximize $f(x,y) = xy$ (area)
  • Constraint: $g(x,y) = 2x + 2y - p = 0$ (perimeter)

Gradients

$$\nabla f = \langle y,\, x\rangle, \qquad \nabla g = \langle 2,\, 2\rangle$$

Lagrange System: $\nabla f = \lambda\,\nabla g$

Component equations:

$$y = 2\lambda \qquad (1)$$ $$x = 2\lambda \qquad (2)$$ $$2x + 2y = p \qquad (3)$$

Solving

From (1) and (2): $x = y = 2\lambda$. Substituting into (3):

$$2(2\lambda) + 2(2\lambda) = p \implies \lambda = \frac{p}{8}, \quad x = y = \frac{p}{4}$$
Conclusion: The only critical point has $x = y = p/4$, so the rectangle is a square with area $A = p^2/16$. $\square$

Triangle of Maximum Area — Heron's Formula

Problem 5 — Proof

Use Lagrange multipliers to prove that the triangle with maximum area for a given perimeter $p$ is equilateral.

Hint: Use Heron's formula $A = \sqrt{s(s-x)(s-y)(s-z)}$ where $s = p/2$ and $x, y, z$ are the side lengths.

Givens

  • $x, y, z$ = side lengths, $s = p/2$ (semi-perimeter, a constant)
  • Objective: maximize $A^2 = s(s-x)(s-y)(s-z)$ (equivalent to maximizing $A$)
  • Constraint: $x + y + z = 2s$ (perimeter)

Substitution

Let $u = s - x$, $v = s - y$, $w = s - z$. Then:

$$u + v + w = 3s - (x+y+z) = 3s - 2s = s$$

Maximize $f(u,v,w) = s \cdot u\,v\,w$ subject to $g(u,v,w) = u + v + w - s = 0$.

Lagrange System

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial u} = s\,vw = \lambda, \qquad \frac{\partial f}{\partial v} = s\,uw = \lambda, \qquad \frac{\partial f}{\partial w} = s\,uv = \lambda$$

Solving

From the first two equations: $s\,vw = s\,uw \implies v = u$ (since $w, s > 0$).

From the last two: $s\,uw = s\,uv \implies w = v$.

So $u = v = w = s/3$, which means:

$$x = s - u = s - \frac{s}{3} = \frac{2s}{3} = \frac{p}{3}$$

and likewise $y = z = p/3$.

Conclusion: All three sides are equal, so the triangle is equilateral with area $A = \dfrac{p^2\sqrt{3}}{36}$. $\square$

The AM-GM Inequality

Why this matters: The AM-GM inequality is one of the most fundamental results in mathematics — it appears everywhere from competition problems to information theory to financial modeling. What makes this proof remarkable is that it shows AM-GM is not just an algebraic trick, but a geometric fact about constrained optimization: the geometric mean is maximized exactly when all variables are equal, and its maximum value is the arithmetic mean.
Problem 6

(a) Find the maximum value of $f(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n) = \sqrt[n]{x_1 x_2 \cdots x_n}$ given that $x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n$ are positive and $x_1 + x_2 + \cdots + x_n = c$.

(b) Deduce the AM-GM inequality: for positive numbers $x_1, \ldots, x_n$,

$$\sqrt[n]{x_1 x_2 \cdots x_n} \leq \frac{x_1 + x_2 + \cdots + x_n}{n}$$

Under what circumstances are these two means equal?

Part (a) — Givens

  • $x_1, \ldots, x_n > 0$
  • Objective: maximize $f = x_1 x_2 \cdots x_n$ (equivalent to maximizing the $n$-th root)
  • Constraint: $g = x_1 + x_2 + \cdots + x_n - c = 0$

Lagrange System

For each $i = 1, \ldots, n$:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i} = \prod_{j \neq i} x_j = \lambda \cdot 1 = \lambda$$

Solving

Setting any two partial derivatives equal:

$$\prod_{j \neq 1} x_j = \prod_{j \neq 2} x_j \implies x_2 = x_1$$

Repeating for all pairs: $x_1 = x_2 = \cdots = x_n$. From the constraint: $x_i = c/n$ for all $i$.

Maximum value of the geometric mean: $$\sqrt[n]{(c/n)^n} = \frac{c}{n}$$

Part (b) — Deduction

For any positive $x_1, \ldots, x_n$, set $c = x_1 + \cdots + x_n$. Part (a) says the geometric mean is maximized at $c/n$, so:

$$\sqrt[n]{x_1 x_2 \cdots x_n} \leq \frac{c}{n} = \frac{x_1 + x_2 + \cdots + x_n}{n}$$
Equality holds if and only if $x_1 = x_2 = \cdots = x_n$.

The Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality

Why this matters: The Cauchy-Schwarz inequality is arguably the single most useful inequality in all of mathematics. It underpins the triangle inequality in every inner product space, proves that correlation coefficients lie in $[-1,1]$, and is the backbone of countless results in linear algebra, probability, and physics. Here, Lagrange multipliers with two constraints give a clean, elegant proof — and the two multipliers $\lambda$, $\mu$ are forced to be $\pm 1/2$.
Problem 7

(a) Maximize $\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i y_i$ subject to $\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i^2 = 1$ and $\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n} y_i^2 = 1$.

(b) Substitute $x_i = \dfrac{a_i}{\sqrt{\sum a_j^2}}$ and $y_i = \dfrac{b_i}{\sqrt{\sum b_j^2}}$ to prove the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality:

$$\left|\sum a_i b_i\right| \leq \sqrt{\sum a_i^2}\;\sqrt{\sum b_i^2}$$

Part (a) — Givens

  • Objective: maximize $f = \sum x_i y_i$
  • Constraint 1: $g_1 = \sum x_i^2 - 1 = 0$ (unit sphere in $x$-space)
  • Constraint 2: $g_2 = \sum y_i^2 - 1 = 0$ (unit sphere in $y$-space)
  • Two constraints $\Rightarrow$ two multipliers $\lambda, \mu$

Lagrange System

For each $i$:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i} = y_i = 2\lambda x_i \qquad (1)$$ $$\frac{\partial f}{\partial y_i} = x_i = 2\mu y_i \qquad (2)$$

Solving

Substitute (1) into (2): $x_i = 2\mu(2\lambda x_i) = 4\lambda\mu\, x_i$, so $4\lambda\mu = 1$.

From (1): $\sum y_i^2 = 4\lambda^2 \sum x_i^2$, i.e. $1 = 4\lambda^2$, giving $\lambda = \pm\tfrac{1}{2}$.

$\lambda$Relationship$\sum x_i y_i$Result
$+1/2$$y_i = x_i$$\sum x_i^2 = 1$Maximum
$-1/2$$y_i = -x_i$$-\sum x_i^2 = -1$Minimum
On the unit spheres: $-1 \leq \sum x_i y_i \leq 1$, with equality iff $\mathbf{y} = \pm\mathbf{x}$.

Part (b) — Deduction

Set $x_i = \dfrac{a_i}{\sqrt{\sum a_j^2}}$ and $y_i = \dfrac{b_i}{\sqrt{\sum b_j^2}}$. These satisfy $\sum x_i^2 = \sum y_i^2 = 1$, so by part (a):

$$\frac{\sum a_i b_i}{\sqrt{\sum a_i^2}\;\sqrt{\sum b_i^2}} \leq 1$$
$$\boxed{\left|\sum a_i b_i\right| \leq \sqrt{\sum a_i^2}\;\sqrt{\sum b_i^2}} \qquad \text{(Cauchy-Schwarz)}$$

Equality holds if and only if $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$ are proportional, i.e. $a_i = k\,b_i$ for some constant $k$.

Deriving OLS Regression via Calculus

Why this matters: Ordinary least squares (OLS) is the foundation of linear regression — the single most widely used statistical method in science, economics, medicine, and machine learning. Every time someone fits a line to data, they are (often without knowing it) solving exactly the optimization problem below. The formulas for $\hat{\beta}_0$ and $\hat{\beta}_1$ that fall out are the same ones used by every statistics package in the world. Deriving them from scratch with calculus shows that the "best-fit line" is not a heuristic — it is a theorem.
Problem 8 — Interactive

Given data points $(x_1, y_1), \ldots, (x_n, y_n)$, find the line $\hat{y} = \hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_1 x$ that minimizes the sum of squared residuals:

$$S(\beta_0, \beta_1) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} (y_i - \beta_0 - \beta_1 x_i)^2$$
data points regression line squared residuals

Givens

  • $n$ data points $(x_i, y_i)$
  • $\bar{x} = \frac{1}{n}\sum x_i$, $\bar{y} = \frac{1}{n}\sum y_i$ (sample means)
  • Objective: minimize $S(\beta_0, \beta_1) = \sum (y_i - \beta_0 - \beta_1 x_i)^2$

Setting up the system: $\nabla S = \vec{0}$

Take partial derivatives and set them to zero:

$$\frac{\partial S}{\partial \beta_0} = -2\sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i - \beta_0 - \beta_1 x_i) = 0 \qquad (1)$$ $$\frac{\partial S}{\partial \beta_1} = -2\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i(y_i - \beta_0 - \beta_1 x_i) = 0 \qquad (2)$$

Solving for $\hat{\beta}_0$ from equation (1)

Expanding (1):

$$\sum y_i - n\beta_0 - \beta_1 \sum x_i = 0$$
$$\hat{\beta}_0 = \bar{y} - \hat{\beta}_1 \bar{x}$$

The regression line always passes through $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$.

Solving for $\hat{\beta}_1$ from equation (2)

Substituting $\hat{\beta}_0 = \bar{y} - \hat{\beta}_1\bar{x}$ into (2) and simplifying:

$$\sum x_i y_i - n\bar{x}\bar{y} - \hat{\beta}_1\!\left(\sum x_i^2 - n\bar{x}^2\right) = 0$$
$$\boxed{\hat{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \bar{x})^2} = \frac{S_{xy}}{S_{xx}}}$$

Confirming it's a minimum (Hessian check)

The Hessian of $S$ is:

$$H = 2\begin{pmatrix} n & \sum x_i \\ \sum x_i & \sum x_i^2 \end{pmatrix}$$

$\det(H) = 4\!\left(n\sum x_i^2 - \left(\sum x_i\right)^2\right) = 4n\sum(x_i - \bar{x})^2 > 0$ (as long as the $x_i$ are not all identical), and the diagonal entries are positive. So $H$ is positive definite — confirming a minimum.

Example: data $(1,2),\ (2,4),\ (3,5),\ (4,4),\ (5,5)$

$\bar{y}$$4$
$S_{xy} = \sum(x_i-\bar{x})(y_i-\bar{y})$$6$
$S_{xx} = \sum(x_i-\bar{x})^2$$10$
$\hat{\beta}_1 = S_{xy}/S_{xx}$$0.6$

Problem: The Piriform Cusp

Problem 5

Maximize $f(x,y) = x$ on the piriform curve $y^2 + x^4 - x^3 = 0$.

Lagrange finds $x=1, y=0$ (or nearby). But the cusp at the origin $(0,0)$ is the true minimum of $x$ on the curve.

$\nabla g = \vec{0}$ at the origin. The piriform has a cusp there — no well-defined tangent. Lagrange is blind to this point.
